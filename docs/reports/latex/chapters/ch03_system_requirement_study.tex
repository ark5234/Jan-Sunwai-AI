\chapter{System Requirement Study}

This chapter explains the system requirements of Jan-Sunwai AI as the fundamentals. It includes active grievance mechanisms, user characteristics, actor expectations, and formalized functional and non-functional requirements. The analysis was based on the realities of implementation, observation of municipal workflow, and assumptions testing on user journeys.

\section{Existing System Overview}

The traditional ways of submitting civic complaints are through one or more of the following interfaces: physical counters, web forms, call centre logging, and general ticketing portals. These can be used for complaints to be raised but have disadvantages of poor input quality, manual routing, low traceability and high staff dependency.

The following are some of the key observed characteristics of existing systems:

\begin{itemize}
\item Citizens must choose the departments themselves, without proper guidance.
\item Complaint narratives are very text-heavy, lack organization and vary in terms of quality of detail.
\item Photographic evidence is accepted but is not a common source of routing information.
\item No status updates are done frequently or in real time.
\item Audit trails do not contain meta-information regarding the change: who changed what and why.
\item Historical retrieval and reporting is challenging.
\end{itemize}

The vulnerabilities found on civic complaint sites can be broken down into several different sections. The most structurally significant weakness is citizen-driven department selection which passes the responsibility for department classification to the one least able to carry it out properly. Any complaints received in the wrong department will need to be reviewed before any action can be taken and each transfer takes a full review cycle. When addressed to the staff member in an unstructured free-text field, the quality of the complaint varies considerably, and sometimes complaints are so poorly formulated that they are only minimally factual, necessitating further questioning by staff to enable them to take field action. When accepted, photographic evidence is treated as an attachment and not as a routing input which is the most objective record of the issue used in classification. A lack of interface design for roles leads to the same interface for field workers, department supervisors, and system administrators, but with vastly different information needs. Lifecycle traceability is not sufficient for governance as there is no actor attribution for state transitions and timestamps. There is no support for escalation justification and/or post-resolution review, and no public visibility of the behaviour of queues, leading to a loss of citizen trust over time.

\section{User Characteristics}

The requirements analysis identified 4 distinct operational personas which have characteristic interaction patterns, and well defined authorisation boundaries.

The citizen is the biggest user group. They deal with the platform during two phases, Submission and tracking. On submission, a citizen posts the photographic evidence and then reads the AI-generated version of the complaint and (optionally) updates the description or category and then submits. The citizen can get notified via notification panel and the complaint details view at tracking.

The platform is used mostly by Field Workers via their assigned task queue. When a field intervention is successful, a worker will update their availability status, view details and location of a complaint, and will record the resolution in a worker's note marking the complaint as complete.

The complaint queue is under the general supervision of the Department Head. They update their status with explanatory notes, escalate complaints to other departments if the analysis of jurisdiction suggests that they are misassigned and to parent authorities if they are beyond their scope of or competency.

Administrators have end-to-end governance rights. They can accept or deny applications for workers to register, accept the triage queue of known low-confidence AI classifications, execute high-volume status operations on complaint backlogs, export complaint datasets as CSV files, and view system-wide analytics.

\section{Hardware and Software Requirements}

To function, at least 6 cores (8 cores is preferred), 16 GB of system RAM, 4-6 GB of GPU VRAM, 10-20 GB of storage and a reliable Internet connection are required. Requirements include a computer running the Windows 10 operating system or Ubuntu 22.04 LTS or higher with Python 3.10 or higher, Node.js v20 LTS or higher, MongoDB 7.x, Ollama runtime, Docker and Compose 24 or above and a modern web browser.

\section{Constraints}

There are governance and usability constraints in addition to the technical one of the project. The platform should be secure, role-separated and should leverage existing civic security, infrastructure and role separation constraints without compromising clarity for citizens and efficiency for officials.

\subsection{UI Constraints}

Access is limited to boundaries on role specific pages. Interfaces are designed to fit mobile widths as low as 375 px, and refresh a page without losing data state, enabling the user to regain context upon reconnect.

\subsection{Communication Interface}

Communication is limited to authenticated rest endpoints and is only made with secure cookie sessions; routing is done through /api/v1 proxy and CORS policy is limited.

\subsection{Hardware Interface}

Compared to an open system deployment, local inference is constrained by the VRAM available on the host machine and so this can result in variable AI response times as load increases. Large file uploads are heavily limited and checked for size and content to prevent any needless resources.

\subsection{Criticality of Application}

The application informs about public complaints and plays an important role for civic accountability. The city's response in the field will be delayed and citizens' confidence in services will erode if there is a prolonged outage.

\subsection{Safety and Security Considerations}

It operates on local infrastructure to maintain citizens' trust and data privacy. Status history and role checks are permanent. Input sanitisation, MIME-type validation, strong token security headers, and lifecycle controls all help to provide defence-in-depth.

\section{Assumptions and Dependencies}

There are several assumptions that underlie the system. First, there's a need for a reachable, healthy MongoDB instance to support all read/write persistence. Second, the Ollama runtime needs to be properly configured to have the right vision and reasoning models for image analysis and the generation of a text draft. Thirdly, environment variables need to be set properly for stable authentication, CORS, database mapping etc. Fourth, the system assumes that information about the workers, such as their department and service area metadata, is complete for the auto-assignment engine to work properly. Lastly, environments must be reproducible in the production deployments, which means that the host must have Docker and Compose installed.

The MoSCoW approach is used for categorising requirements. These are some of the Must-Have features that are essential for the viability of operations: User registration and authentication, complaint intake, status updates, auto-assignment and health monitoring. Together, these Should-Have features like, authority escalation, worker status controls, the administrative triage queue and analytics helps deliver governance at institutional grade. Because of Can-Do, CSV exports and the public transparency feed are added to the main workflow without disruption, with high value reporting. Requirements that are not associated with any functionality, like session security, input sanitisation, observability, are not considered value added but as a minimum baseline control.

\section{Chapter Summary}

The requirements analysis identified the need for a switch from manual grievance handling to a more secure, structured and auditable digital process. Functional and non-functional requirements were placed in terms of deliverable, implementable requirements, realistic user roles were translated to operational constraints and MoSCoW methodology was used to prioritize the delivery.
